Now that you understand what LLMs are and their capabilities, it's time to dissect the building blocks of an effective prompt. Every great AI output starts with a well-structured input. This lesson teaches you the fundamental components of a prompt and how to combine them for maximum impact.

The Four Core Components of a Prompt

Think of a prompt like a recipe. Each ingredient serves a specific purpose, and the way you combine them determines the quality of the final dish.

1. Context (The "Who" and "Why")

Context provides the background information the model needs to understand the situation. It sets the stage for the task.

Example:

"I am a small business owner launching an eco-friendly skincare line targeted at millennials who value sustainability."

Why it matters: Without context, the model has to guess your situation. The more specific you are, the more tailored the response.

  • Who are you? (student, CEO, developer, parent)
  • What's the scenario? (launching a product, writing a thesis, debugging code)
  • Why does this matter? (deadline pressure, high stakes, learning goal)

2. Instruction (The "What")

The instruction is the core task you want the model to perform. Be explicit and action-oriented.

Weak Instruction:

"Tell me about marketing."

Strong Instruction:

"Write a 300-word Instagram caption announcing the launch of my new eco-friendly face serum, highlighting its key ingredients and sustainability benefits."

Action verbs to use:

  • Write, summarize, explain, analyze, compare, generate, translate, debug, outline, rewrite

3. Constraints (The "How")

Constraints define the boundaries: format, length, tone, style, and any limitations.

Example Constraints:

  • Length: "Keep it under 200 words" or "Provide 5 bullet points"
  • Tone: "Use a professional, formal tone" or "Write in a friendly, conversational style"
  • Format: "Structure as a numbered list" or "Use markdown headings"
  • Audience: "Explain this to a 10-year-old" or "Target C-level executives"

Pro Tip: The more constraints you add, the more focused and relevant the output becomes.

4. Examples (The "Show Me")

Providing examples dramatically improves output quality. This is called "few-shot prompting" (we'll dive deeper in Module 3).

Example with Demonstration:

"Convert the following product names to catchy taglines:

Product: EcoWash Detergent → Tagline: 'Clean clothes, cleaner planet'

Product: SolarCharge Battery → Tagline: 'Power that never sleeps'

Now do the same for: Product: HydroGlow Face Cream"

Putting It All Together: The Formula

Basic Prompt Formula:

[Context] + [Instruction] + [Constraints] + [Examples]

Not all prompts need all four components, but including them increases precision.

Real-World Example: Complete Prompt

Scenario: You're a HR manager creating a job posting.

[Context] I am an HR manager at a fast-growing tech startup specializing in AI tools for healthcare. We're hiring for a senior software engineer position.

[Instruction] Write a compelling job posting that will attract top-tier engineering talent.

[Constraints] Keep it to 400 words max. Use an enthusiastic but professional tone. Include sections for: Role Overview, Key Responsibilities, Required Qualifications, and Why Join Us. Avoid corporate jargon.

[Examples] Use action phrases like "You'll be building..." instead of "The candidate will be responsible for..."

Common Prompt Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake Why It's Bad Better Approach
Being too vague Forces model to guess your intent Add specific details and context
Asking multiple questions at once Model may only answer one part Break into separate prompts or number your questions
No output format specified Response may be hard to use Request specific format (bullets, table, paragraph)
Assuming the model "knows" you Model has no memory between sessions Always provide fresh context each time

Mini Exercise: Build Your First Prompt

Exercise Task:

You need to create social media content for a fictional coffee shop called "Brew Haven" that just started offering oat milk alternatives. Build a complete prompt using all four components.

Your Turn:

  1. Context: Who are you? What's the situation?
  2. Instruction: What exactly do you want created?
  3. Constraints: Length? Tone? Format? Audience?
  4. Examples: Show a style or format you want mimicked
Click to see a sample solution

I am the social media manager for Brew Haven, a cozy neighborhood coffee shop in Portland known for its artisanal coffee and community vibe. We just introduced oat milk as a dairy alternative. Write an engaging Instagram post (max 150 words) announcing this new option. Use a warm, friendly tone with emoji. Structure it as: Hook (question or statement), announcement, benefit, call-to-action. Example tone: "☕ Coffee lovers, your mornings just got creamier! Introducing our new oat milk option..."

Key Takeaway:

A well-structured prompt is like giving someone a detailed map instead of just saying "go north." The more guidance you provide, the closer you get to your desired destination.
