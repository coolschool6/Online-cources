Constraints are the guardrails that keep AI output focused, relevant, and usable. This lesson teaches you how to use limitations—length, format, tone, audience, and scope—to dramatically improve output precision.

Why Constraints Matter

Without constraints, the AI has infinite possibilities and will choose based on statistical probability, not your actual needs. Constraints eliminate ambiguity and ensure the output matches your exact requirements.

Think of it like this:

Asking "Write about coffee" could give you a history essay, a scientific paper, a poem, or a product description. Asking "Write a 100-word Instagram caption about coffee in a playful tone" gives you exactly what you need.

The 6 Essential Constraint Types

1. Length Constraints

Control output size for specific use cases:

  • "Write a 300-word blog introduction"
  • "Summarize this in 3 bullet points"
  • "Create a 60-second elevator pitch (approximately 150 words)"
  • "Provide 5 examples, each no more than one sentence"
  • "Keep the response under 2 paragraphs"

Pro Tip: Be specific. "Short" is subjective; "200 words" is precise.

2. Format Constraints

Dictate structure for easy consumption or direct usability:

Common Formats:

  • Lists: "Provide a numbered list of 10 steps"
  • Tables: "Create a comparison table with columns for Price, Features, and Best Use Case"
  • Code: "Output as JSON format" or "Write Python code with comments"
  • Markdown: "Use markdown formatting with H2 headings and bullet points"
  • Email: "Structure as a professional email with subject line, greeting, body, and signature"
  • Outline: "Create a hierarchical outline with main topics and subtopics"

3. Tone Constraints

Set the emotional and stylistic register:

Tone When to Use Example Phrase
Professional/Formal Business docs, academic writing "Use formal, corporate language"
Conversational/Friendly Social media, blogs, newsletters "Write in a warm, approachable tone"
Persuasive/Urgent Sales copy, calls-to-action "Use compelling, action-oriented language"
Technical/Precise Documentation, specifications "Be technical and exact; avoid metaphors"
Playful/Witty Creative content, brand voice "Use humor and wordplay"
Empathetic/Supportive Customer service, healthcare "Be compassionate and reassuring"

4. Audience Constraints

Tailor complexity and references to your target reader:

  • "Explain this to a complete beginner with no technical background"
  • "Write for C-suite executives who need high-level insights"
  • "Target parents of elementary school children"
  • "Address software developers familiar with Python"
  • "Write for skeptical journalists who will fact-check every claim"

5. Scope Constraints

Define boundaries for what to include or exclude:

Inclusion Constraints:

  • "Focus only on renewable energy sources"
  • "Include at least 3 statistical citations"
  • "Must mention our brand values: sustainability and transparency"

Exclusion Constraints:

  • "Do not mention competitors by name"
  • "Avoid technical jargon or acronyms"
  • "Don't discuss pricing or cost"

6. Style Constraints

Specify writing style, voice, or reference models:

  • "Write in the style of The New York Times"
  • "Mimic the tone of Apple's product descriptions"
  • "Use short, punchy sentences like Hemingway"
  • "Adopt a storytelling approach with a narrative arc"

Combining Multiple Constraints

Master Prompt Example:

"Write a 250-word LinkedIn post (Length) announcing our new product launch. Use a professional but enthusiastic tone (Tone). Structure it with an attention-grabbing opening question, followed by 3 bullet points highlighting benefits, and end with a clear call-to-action (Format). Target B2B decision-makers in healthcare (Audience). Focus on security and compliance benefits (Scope). Avoid buzzwords and corporate jargon (Style)."

This single prompt combines 6 constraint types for maximum precision.

Real-World Application: Job Description

Scenario: Writing a Job Posting

Bad prompt: "Write a job description for a marketing manager."

Good prompt with constraints:

"Write a job description for a Senior Marketing Manager position at a fast-growing SaaS startup. Keep it to 400 words max (Length). Use an enthusiastic, modern tone that appeals to ambitious professionals (Tone). Structure it in 4 sections: Role Overview (2 sentences), Key Responsibilities (5 bullet points), Required Qualifications (5 bullet points), Why Join Us (3 bullet points) (Format). Target candidates with 7-10 years of B2B marketing experience (Audience). Emphasize growth opportunity, ownership, and impact. Avoid clichés like 'rockstar' or 'ninja' (Style). Do not mention salary range (Scope)."

Common Mistakes with Constraints

Mistake Why It's Problematic Fix
Too many contradictory constraints Model gets confused Prioritize and simplify
Vague constraints ("keep it short") Open to interpretation Be specific ("max 150 words")
No format constraint for complex output Hard to parse or use Specify structure (list, table, etc.)
Unrealistic constraints Model can't meet requirements Balance detail with feasibility

Key Takeaway:

Constraints are not limitations—they're instructions for excellence. The more precisely you define what you want, the less the model has to guess. Every constraint eliminates 1,000 possible wrong answers.
